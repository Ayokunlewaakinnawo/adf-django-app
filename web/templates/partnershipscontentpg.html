Introduction

Data-Pop Alliance is a global nonprofit organization that aims to promote the use of data and technology for sustainable development and social good. The organization was founded in 2013 by a team of experts in data science, technology, and development, including Emmanuel Letouzé, Alex 'Sandy' Pentland, and Patrick Vinck.
Data-Pop Alliance works with governments, NGOs, academic institutions, and the private sector to provide research and analysis on various social and economic issues, including poverty, health, education, and climate change. The organization also provides technical assistance and capacity-building support to help organizations and governments harness the power of data to inform policy and decision-making.
Data-Pop Alliance has several flagship initiatives, including the "Data for Climate Action" program, which aims to use data and technology to support climate change mitigation and adaptation efforts, and the "Data-PopX" program, which provides training and mentorship to emerging data scientists in low- and middle-income countries.
Data-Pop Alliance has partnerships with several organizations, including the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP), the World Economic Forum (WEF), and the International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC). The organization is headquartered in New York City, with offices in Paris and Bogotá.

Who is Dr. Victoria Lemieux?

Dr. Victoria Lemieux is an internationally recognized expert in the field of blockchain, data governance, and information management. She is currently a professor of archival science and the associate dean of research at the University of British Columbia (UBC) in Canada.
Dr. Lemieux has a background in law, with a focus on freedom of information and privacy issues. She has worked as a policy analyst for the Canadian government and has also served as a consultant for various organizations, including the World Bank, the UN, and the International Records Management Trust.
‍
Dr. Lemieux's research focuses on the use of blockchain technology for data governance and management, with a particular emphasis on how blockchain can be used to support the preservation and accessibility of archival records. She has published numerous papers and articles on these topics and is a sought-after speaker at conferences and events around the world.
‍
Dr. Lemieux is also the founder and director of the Blockchain@UBC research cluster, which brings together researchers, industry partners, and students to explore the potential of blockchain technology for social and economic development. She is a recipient of several awards and honors, including the Digital Preservation Coalition (DPC) Fellowship and the Emmett Leahy Award for her contributions to the field of records management.

What is data governance?

Dr. Nii Quaynor
Dr. Bitango Ndemo
Dr. Tunde Adegbola

Data governance refers to the management of data-related policies, procedures, and practices within an organization. It involves setting up frameworks and processes to ensure that data is managed effectively and in compliance with relevant laws and regulations.
‍
Data governance encompasses a range of activities, including data quality management, data security and privacy, data sharing and access, and data lifecycle management. It involves establishing clear guidelines and standards for data management, assigning responsibilities for data stewardship, and ensuring that data is managed in a consistent and coordinated way across the organization.
‍
Effective data governance helps organizations to ensure that data is accurate, reliable, and accessible when needed. It also helps to ensure that data is managed in compliance with relevant laws and regulations, protecting the organization from legal and reputational risks.
Data governance is particularly important in the context of big data, where organizations are dealing with increasingly large volumes of complex data from a variety of sources. Good data governance practices can help organizations to effectively manage this data and derive meaningful insights from it.

Robust data collection is essential for many applications, including scientific research, business decision-making, and public policy. The following are some of the drivers for robust data collection:
1. Accuracy: Collecting accurate data is critical to ensuring that any conclusions drawn from the data are reliable. Accurate data also helps to identify patterns, trends, and relationships that may not be apparent from incomplete or inaccurate data.
‍
2. Reliability: Data collected from a variety of sources should be consistent, reliable, and repeatable. This ensures that the data can be trusted and used to make informed decisions.
‍
3. Validity: Validity refers to the extent to which data measures what it is intended to measure. For example, if a survey is designed to measure satisfaction with a particular product, it should ask questions that are relevant to the product and the satisfaction of the user.
‍
4. Timeliness: Data collected in a timely manner is often more useful than data collected after a long delay. Timely data can help decision-makers to respond quickly to emerging trends and issues.
‍
5. Completeness: Complete data is essential for making informed decisions. Missing data can lead to biased conclusions and inaccurate predictions.
‍
6. Consistency: Data collected using consistent methods and procedures ensures that the data is comparable and can be used to draw meaningful comparisons over time.
‍
7. Ethics: Data collection should be conducted in an ethical manner that respects the privacy and autonomy of participants. This includes obtaining informed consent, protecting the confidentiality of data, and avoiding harm or exploitation.Overall, robust data collection is critical for ensuring that the data is accurate, reliable, valid, timely, complete, consistent, and ethical, and can be used to make informed decisions.

Relationships can be classified in various ways depending on the context, nature, and characteristics of the relationship. Here are some of the common ways relationships can be classified:
Based on nature: Relationships can be classified based on the nature of the connection between two individuals. For example, romantic relationships, familial relationships, and professional relationships are all different types of relationships based on the nature of the connection.
Based on duration: Relationships can be classified based on the duration of the connection between two individuals. For example, short-term relationships, long-term relationships, and lifelong relationships are all different types of relationships based on the duration of the connection.
Based on power dynamic: Relationships can be classified based on the power dynamic between two individuals. For example, parent-child relationships, employer-employee relationships, and teacher-student relationships are all different types of relationships based on the power dynamic.
Based on intimacy: Relationships can be classified based on the level of intimacy between two individuals. For example, close friendships, casual friendships, and acquaintanceships are all different types of relationships based on the level of intimacy.
Based on communication: Relationships can be classified based on the level of communication between two individuals. For example, high communication relationships, low communication relationships, and no communication relationships are all different types of relationships based on the level of communication.
Based on reciprocity: Relationships can be classified based on the level of reciprocity between two individuals. For example, reciprocal relationships, one-sided relationships, and exploitative relationships are all different types of relationships based on the level of reciprocity.
